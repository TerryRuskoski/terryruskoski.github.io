# Building a Resilient Lab Inventory and Procurement System

*Example from research lab operations during my PhD*

## Overview

Early in my graduate work, I observed that our lab’s inventory and ordering system--while recently improved from handwritten requests to a digital platform--still failed to reliably meet day-to-day operational needs. Critical communal supplies were frequently out of stock, and borrowing from neighboring labs had become the default workaround.

When the primary inventory manager graduated, I took ownership of the system and redesigned it to improve reliability, visibility, and resilience. This included standardizing procurement workflows, implementing inventory controls, and improving physical organization within the lab.

The result was a sustainable system that supported uninterrupted lab operations, adapted to supply chain disruptions, and could be successfully transferred across team members.

## Impact

- Eliminated chronic stockouts of high-use lab supplies  
- Reduced reliance on inter-lab borrowing as a default operating mode  
- Established a **predictable, resilient inventory system**, including during COVID-related supply disruptions  
- Enabled full team adoption through **SOP-driven training and role rotation**  
- Created documentation that was adopted by researchers outside the lab

## Operational Scope

- **System type:** Lab inventory and procurement workflow  
- **Users:** Multi-member research lab with shared consumables  
- **Environment:** Academic lab transitioning to a new institutional procurement system  
- **Constraints:** Limited training resources, variable supply lead times, shared inventory across users  
- **Failure mode:** Frequent stockouts, inconsistent ordering practices, and lack of system ownership  

## Challenge

The lab had already transitioned from handwritten inventory requests to a digital platform (Quartzy), reducing ordering errors. However, key operational issues remained:

- Frequently depleted communal supplies, requiring constant borrowing from other labs  
- No standardized process for monitoring inventory levels or anticipating demand  
- Lack of training resources for a newly implemented institutional procurement system  
- Heavy reliance on a single individual for ordering, creating continuity risk  

This resulted in a system that was digitized but **not operationally reliable**, with persistent gaps in inventory management and process ownership.


## Approach
I approached the problem as a system design challenge, focusing on reliability, visibility, and transferability:

> How can inventory management be structured so that supply levels are predictable, responsibilities are distributed, and the system functions independently of any single individual?

Key steps included:
- Defining inventory management as a repeatable workflow with clear **inputs, checkpoints, and outputs**  
- Establishing minimum reorder points based on usage patterns and supplier lead times  
- Designing a low-cost, behavior-driven system to make reorder points visible and actionable to all users  
- Implementing regular inventory audits through structured lab walkthroughs  
- Creating end-user-focused SOPs for the institutional procurement system, filling gaps left by inadequate training materials  
- Improving physical organization and visibility of supplies to reduce friction in daily use  

## Process Improvements Implemented
- Developed detailed **SOP documentation** for procurement workflows tailored to research staff  
- Created **inventory control rules**, including minimum reorder thresholds for high-use items  
- Implemented **visual reorder cues directly on inventory packaging** (e.g., “reorder at this line,” “reorder two when empty”) to distribute responsibility across users  
- Instituted **weekly inventory checks** to proactively manage supply levels  
- Standardized ordering through integration of **Quartzy and institutional procurement systems**  
- Designed a **peer-training model** (“learn → execute → train”) to scale knowledge across the lab  
- Improved physical organization by implementing **labeled storage systems** for shared materials  

## Outcome
- Transitioned the system from reactive (stockouts and borrowing) to **proactive inventory management**  
- **Reduced reliance on a single individual** by embedding inventory awareness into daily workflows 
- Maintained consistent supply availability, even during **COVID-related supply chain disruptions**  
- Enabled rapid onboarding of new users through clear documentation and training workflows  
- Successfully transferred system ownership from a single individual to a **rotating team-based model**  
- Created a system robust enough to support not only the lab, but also neighboring groups during shortages  

## Long-Term Impact
- Established a sustainable inventory system that remained in use after role transition  
- Demonstrated that operational reliability depends on both process design and physical system clarity 
- Highlighted the importance of aligning digital tools with real-world workflows
- Provided a scalable model for managing shared resources in multi-user environments  
- Reinforced the value of training systems that propagate knowledge through teams rather than relying on central ownership

## Skills Demonstrated

- Operational workflow design and process standardization  
- Inventory management and demand planning under uncertainty  
- Root cause identification in system inefficiencies  
- SOP development and documentation for non-expert users  
- Cross-functional coordination and informal leadership  
- Training system design and knowledge transfer  
- Adapting systems to external constraints (e.g., supply chain disruption)  

## Reflection

This experience reinforced that operational systems do not fail solely due to lack of tools, but often due to gaps in structure, ownership, and visibility.

Notably, effective solutions do not always require new resources—many of the most impactful improvements came from designing systems that aligned with how people naturally interact with their environment. By embedding reorder signals directly into the physical workspace, inventory management became a shared, low-friction responsibility rather than a centralized task.

This same approach—designing workflows that anticipate failure points, align with user behavior, and distribute ownership—is directly applicable to operational environments in industry settings.
