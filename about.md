# About Me

I’m a systems thinker who is driven to understand how complex environments function—and why they sometimes don’t.

My path hasn’t been linear, but the throughline has been consistent.

I began with a BA in Sociology, where I was trained to think about systems at the human level—how people interact with structures, where breakdowns occur, and how environments shape behavior. I then moved into healthcare and later into research, ultimately earning a PhD in Biochemistry.

While the domains changed, my approach did not.

Across every environment I’ve worked in, I’ve found myself drawn to the same questions:

- *How is this system supposed to work?*
- *Where is it breaking down?*  
- *What’s missing, unclear, or unnecessarily complex?*  
- *How can it be redesigned so it actually works for the people using it?*  

During my PhD, this showed up in both scientific and operational contexts. I studied complex molecular systems, diagnosing missing dependencies and redesigning experimental approaches to restore function. At the same time, I was naturally drawn to improving the systems around me—whether that meant redesigning lab workflows, building inventory systems, or coordinating large, multi-team operations like synchrotron beamtime.

I don’t approach problems by optimizing pieces in isolation. I look at the full system—inputs, dependencies, constraints, and how information and responsibility flow through it—and then redesign it to be more reliable, more intuitive, and more sustainable.

This way of thinking isn’t something I was formally trained to do—it’s how I naturally engage with the world. I notice inefficiencies, question assumptions, and build better structures wherever I am, whether that’s in a lab, a shared workspace, or everyday environments.

During graduate school, I also became a parent, navigating the demands of research alongside raising my son. That experience reinforced the importance of building systems that are not only effective, but resilient—systems that continue to function under real-world constraints, not just ideal conditions.

Now, I’m intentionally pursuing roles where this mindset is not incidental, but central—where I can apply systems thinking, process design, and continuous improvement to help teams operate more effectively.
